DOCUMENT: Lithium + Ibuprofen (NSAIDs) Drug Interaction
SOURCE: BNF 2024 / Clinical Pharmacology Reviews / MHRA Guidance
SEVERITY: MAJOR
DRUG_PAIR: lithium + ibuprofen

INTERACTION SUMMARY
NSAIDs, including ibuprofen, can significantly increase lithium plasma concentrations — potentially to toxic levels — through reduced renal lithium clearance. Lithium has a narrow therapeutic index (therapeutic range 0.6-1.2 mmol/L; toxic above 1.5 mmol/L). This interaction can cause serious lithium toxicity and is considered a major clinical drug interaction by BNF and MHRA. NSAIDs should generally be avoided in patients taking lithium.

MECHANISM
Lithium is eliminated almost entirely by the kidneys. Renal lithium reabsorption closely parallels sodium reabsorption — they compete for the same proximal tubular reabsorption transporters. NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandin synthesis via COX-1 and COX-2 inhibition:
- Prostaglandins (particularly PGE2) normally inhibit sodium (and lithium) reabsorption in the proximal tubule
- NSAID-mediated prostaglandin inhibition → increased sodium (and lithium) reabsorption
- Result: lithium clearance reduced by 15-30%, plasma lithium levels increase by similar proportion
- If a patient is at the upper end of therapeutic range (e.g. 0.9-1.0 mmol/L), a 25% increase brings them into the toxic range (1.1-1.3+ mmol/L)
- Ibuprofen has been specifically documented to cause lithium toxicity through this mechanism

LITHIUM TOXICITY CLINICAL FEATURES
Mild (1.5-2.0 mmol/L): Nausea, diarrhoea, fine tremor, fatigue, polyuria
Moderate (2.0-2.5 mmol/L): Coarse tremor, confusion, muscle twitching, ataxia, slurred speech
Severe (>2.5 mmol/L): Seizures, coma, cardiac arrhythmias, permanent neurological damage, death
Note: Elderly patients and those with renal impairment are at risk at lower plasma levels

DOSE CONSIDERATIONS
- Risk exists at all therapeutic doses of ibuprofen (including OTC doses of 200-400mg)
- Even short-course ibuprofen (3-5 days) can cause clinically significant lithium accumulation
- Dehydration (from illness, hot weather, reduced fluid intake) markedly amplifies the interaction — sodium/lithium retention increases further
- Patient must be warned NOT to take ibuprofen OTC without medical advice

CLINICAL ACTION
- Avoid ibuprofen and all NSAIDs in patients on lithium where possible
- If pain relief is required: use paracetamol — does not affect lithium clearance
- If NSAID is essential: reduce lithium dose by approximately 25% and check lithium level after 4-5 days
- Check lithium level urgently if patient has started any NSAID
- Advise patient to maintain adequate fluid intake, especially during illness or summer
- Warn patient about OTC ibuprofen availability — many patients do not consider OTC medications as 'drugs'

SAFER ALTERNATIVES FOR PAIN/INFLAMMATION
First choice: Paracetamol (acetaminophen) 500-1000mg — no effect on lithium clearance
Second choice: Low-dose aspirin (75-100mg) for cardiovascular prophylaxis — less effect on lithium than full-dose NSAIDs
Avoid: Ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac, celecoxib, indomethacin (all inhibit renal prostaglandins)

DISCLAIMER: This document is for clinical reference only. Drug interaction management must be individualised by a qualified clinician or pharmacist.
