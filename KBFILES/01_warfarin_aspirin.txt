DOCUMENT: Warfarin + Aspirin Drug Interaction
SOURCE: BNF 2024 / CMAJ Population Study (Delaney et al.) / Circulation AHA Guidelines 2021
SEVERITY: MAJOR
DRUG_PAIR: warfarin + aspirin

INTERACTION SUMMARY
Concurrent use of warfarin and aspirin significantly increases the risk of major haemorrhage, including gastrointestinal and intracranial bleeding. This is one of the most clinically significant and well-documented drug interactions in clinical practice. The combination must only be used when there is a compelling clinical indication and with close monitoring.

MECHANISM
Two distinct and additive mechanisms operate simultaneously:
1. PHARMACODYNAMIC: Warfarin inhibits hepatic synthesis of Vitamin K-dependent clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X) and anticoagulant proteins C and S, reducing thrombin generation and prolonging prothrombin time (PT/INR). Aspirin irreversibly inhibits cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1) in platelets, blocking thromboxane A2 synthesis and preventing platelet aggregation for the lifespan of the platelet (7-10 days). These two mechanisms act on different parts of the coagulation cascade and their effects are additive, not merely overlapping.
2. PHARMACOKINETIC (high-dose aspirin only): High-dose aspirin (>3g/day) has a direct hypoprothrombinemic effect by reducing functional Factor VII. This is not relevant at the low-dose aspirin (75-100mg) commonly used in cardiovascular patients.

CLINICAL EVIDENCE
- Population-based cohort study (Delaney et al., CMAJ 2007, n=44,199 cases/controls): Prescribing warfarin with aspirin was associated with an adjusted rate ratio of 6.48 (95% CI 4.25-9.87) for gastrointestinal bleeding compared to warfarin alone.
- Systematic review and meta-analysis (Wang et al., BJCP 2021, 478,334 patients): Concurrent use of aspirin and warfarin significantly increased clinically relevant bleeding (OR 1.50; 95% CI 1.29-1.74) compared to warfarin alone.
- AHA/ACC Guidelines (Circulation 2021): Triple therapy (aspirin + clopidogrel + anticoagulant) should not exceed 30 days and aspirin dose should not exceed 100mg. For AF patients with stable coronary disease, anticoagulation alone (warfarin or DOAC) is preferred.

DOSE CONSIDERATIONS
- Risk is present at ALL doses of aspirin including low-dose (75-100mg daily)
- Risk does not require elevated INR — additive antihaemostatic effect occurs even within therapeutic INR range
- For a 72-year-old patient: Advanced age is an independent risk factor for both bleeding and thromboembolism. Renal function should be assessed. HAS-BLED score should be calculated.

CLINICAL ACTION
- Dual therapy (warfarin + aspirin) should only continue if explicitly advised by a cardiologist/specialist
- If combination is necessary: add proton pump inhibitor (e.g. omeprazole 20mg daily) to reduce GI bleeding risk by approximately 70%
- Increase INR monitoring frequency to weekly when combination is initiated
- Target INR should be maintained at lower end of therapeutic range (2.0-2.5 for AF) where possible
- Educate patient on bleeding signs: unusual bruising, blood in urine/stool, prolonged bleeding from cuts, sudden severe headache
- Review indication for dual therapy at every clinic visit — if aspirin is for secondary prevention and anticoagulation is adequate, aspirin may be discontinued

PATIENT POPULATION CONSIDERATIONS
- Atrial fibrillation: Warfarin alone provides adequate stroke prevention. Aspirin should NOT be added unless patient has concurrent ACS or recent coronary stent
- Post-PCI patients: Minimum dual antiplatelet + anticoagulant duration (30 days), then switch to anticoagulation + single antiplatelet
- Age >65: CHA2DS2-VASc and HAS-BLED scores should both be formally calculated

ALTERNATIVES
- If aspirin is being used for AF stroke prevention: Warfarin alone is superior (BAFTA trial)
- If aspirin is being used for secondary CV prevention: Discuss with cardiologist whether anticoagulation alone is sufficient
- DOAC (apixaban, rivaroxaban) + aspirin carries lower bleeding risk than warfarin + aspirin (ARISTOTLE, ROCKET-AF sub-analyses)

DISCLAIMER: This document is for clinical reference only. Drug interaction management must be individualised by a qualified clinician or pharmacist.
